Risk #1: Federal Energy Policy — What Actually Happened

There's been a lot of noise about IRA funding being "revoked." Here's the precise timeline of what happened and what it means for your business.

The Timeline

DateEventImpact
Jan 20, 2025 Trump signs "Unleashing American Energy" executive order. Pauses disbursement of certain IRA funds for 90-day review. Revokes Biden's EO 14082 (IRA implementation order). Temporary freeze — created uncertainty but courts blocked permanent stops
Jan 23, 2025 White House memo clarifies: pause applies only to programs mentioned in Section 2 of the EO, not all IRA funding. Narrower than feared — HWAP and state rebates not directly targeted
Early 2025 Courts block broad funding freezes. Impoundment Control Act (1974) prevents executive branch from unilaterally stopping congressionally appropriated funds. Legal protection — executive orders cannot override federal statutes
July 4, 2025 "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (OBBBA / Public Law 119-21) signed. This is legislation, not an executive order — it's permanent law. Major impact — this is the real change
Dec 31, 2025 Section 25C (Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit) and Section 25D (Residential Clean Energy Credit) terminated. Dead — no more homeowner tax credits for efficiency upgrades or solar

What's Dead vs. What's Alive

☠️ Terminated by OBBBA (Law)

  • 25C — Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (ended Dec 31, 2025)
  • 25D — Residential Clean Energy Credit (ended Dec 31, 2025)
  • 30D/25E — New & Used EV Credits (ended Sep 30, 2025)
  • 45L — New Energy Efficient Home Credit (ends Jun 30, 2026)
  • 179D — Commercial Building Deduction (ends Jun 30, 2026)

These are gone. Signed into law. Not coming back without new legislation.

✓ Still Available (for now)

  • HWAP — Ohio Home Weatherization Assistance Program (ongoing federal + state funding, separate from IRA)
  • HOMES Rebates — $240M allocated to Ohio, available through Sep 2031
  • HEAR Rebates — Appliance rebates for low/moderate income, through Sep 2031
  • Utility Programs — Columbia Gas WarmChoice, CenterPoint Energy weatherization
  • HEAP/PIPP — Energy assistance programs (Jul 2025 – May 2026 cycle)

These are funded and operational. State-administered, already appropriated. Executive orders can delay but not permanently stop.

⚠️ The Ohio HOMES/HEAR Question Mark Ohio has been allocated $240M and submitted its application to DOE. The state was "awaiting federal approval" as of late 2025 with no clear launch date. The OBBBA did not repeal the HOMES/HEAR programs themselves — they remain funded through 2031. But the current administration has shown willingness to slow-walk approvals. States that already launched (Wisconsin, California, Michigan) are distributing rebates. Ohio may experience delays. Do not build your Year 1 plan around Ohio HOMES/HEAR revenue. Treat it as upside if/when it arrives.

What This Means for Allen Brothers

The Business Case Without Any Federal Programs

Here's the critical question: does this business work if HOMES/HEAR never launches in Ohio and no new tax credits appear? Yes — and here's why:

HWAP is not IRA money. Ohio's Home Weatherization Assistance Program is funded through the Department of Energy's ongoing Weatherization Assistance Program (WAP), which has existed since 1976. It's separate from the IRA. MVCAP serves 600 homes/year across 9 counties with extensive waiting lists. This program pre-dates the IRA by decades and is not affected by OBBBA.

Utility programs are private. Columbia Gas WarmChoice and CenterPoint Energy's weatherization programs are funded by utility ratepayers, not federal programs. They have their own budgets and contractor needs. These are completely insulated from federal policy changes.

The 25C termination actually helps contractors. Here's the counterintuitive part: with homeowner tax credits eliminated, the remaining rebate programs (HOMES, HEAR, utility programs) become the only path to affordable energy upgrades. Demand for certified contractors in these programs increases because there's no DIY tax credit alternative anymore. Homeowners who want savings must go through approved contractor networks.

Risk #2: The Dave Bottleneck

Every opportunity in the market analysis has Dave as the lead builder. He's a journeyman carpenter with OSHA 30, the only brother with hands-on trade skills, and the one doing the physical work. That's the strength of the team — but it's also a single point of failure.

The Math Problem

Dave listed his availability as under 10 hours per week. A typical weatherization job takes 2–4 days of on-site work. A renovation project takes weeks. A flip takes months. Even if Dave goes full-time eventually, one person can only work on one job site at a time.

If Dave is the only one who can do the work, the business has a hard ceiling. Revenue is directly capped by Dave's available hours. He can't take a sick day, can't go on vacation, and every delay at one job cascades to the next. Meanwhile, James and Michael's skills (project management, sales, strategy) can scale infinitely — they can sell and schedule more work than Dave can physically do.

The Real Risk This isn't just a growth problem — it's a Dave burnout problem. If the business succeeds and demand grows (which is the goal), Dave becomes the bottleneck and the pressure increases on the one person with the lowest risk tolerance and the most family concerns. That's a recipe for the exact outcome everyone wants to avoid: business stress damaging the family relationship.

The Hiring Strategy: Building Beyond Dave

Hiring isn't a Phase 3 nice-to-have. It needs to be baked into the business plan from day one, even if the first hire doesn't happen for 6 months.

The Phased Hiring Model

Phase 1 — Months 1–4

Dave as Lead + Subcontractors on Call

Dave does the core work. For jobs that require a second set of hands or specialized skills (HVAC, electrical, plumbing), bring in subcontractors per job. This keeps overhead at zero while building revenue and proving the model.

Dave → Lead installer James → Scheduling + QC Michael → Sub relationships

Key metric: Track Dave's utilization rate. When he's consistently booked 4+ weeks out, it's time for Phase 2.

Phase 2 — Months 4–8

First Hire: Apprentice / Helper

Hire a laborer or apprentice to work alongside Dave. This person handles demo, cleanup, material transport, and basic installation under Dave's supervision. Dave's output roughly doubles because he's not doing low-skill tasks. Dave's wife's concerns note ("Previous business owner") — she may bring valuable hiring perspective.

Dave transitions from doing 100% of the labor to supervising and doing the complex work. Phase 4 — Year 2+

Dave as Quality Lead, Not Daily Laborer

With trained crews, Dave shifts to quality inspections, training new hires, and handling the most complex or high-value work. His shoulder limitations become manageable because he's choosing which jobs need his hands. The business runs without requiring Dave on every site. This is James's 5-year vision: "move into a more passive income situation."

Dave → QC lead + mentor 2–3 Crew members James → Operations manager Michael → Growth + acquisitions

Why This Solves Multiple Problems at Once

For Dave

  • Reduces physical toll (shoulder limitation)
  • No longer single point of failure
  • Can take vacations without stopping revenue
  • Shifts to mentoring — which has no hard "no's" on his list
  • Becomes more like a foreman than a laborer over time

For the Business

  • Revenue scales beyond one person's capacity
  • Can pursue flips + weatherization simultaneously
  • Reduces risk of Dave burnout
  • Aligns with MVCAP's model (they run 9 crews)
  • Ohio needs 15,000+ construction workers — hiring is the market opportunity itself

For Dave's Wife

  • Dave isn't the only one the business depends on
  • His role becomes sustainable, not grueling
  • Clear path to reduced hours, not increased hours
  • The business has an exit plan that doesn't require Dave forever

For James's Flexibility

  • James said he wants to "go hard – go easy – go hard"
  • With crews, the business doesn't stall when James eases off
  • Systems and processes (James's strength) enable this flexibility
  • Crew management is project management — James's superpower

The Key Takeaway These two risks — federal policy and the Dave bottleneck — are actually the same risk wearing different masks. Both are about dependency. Don't depend solely on federal programs for revenue. Don't depend solely on Dave for labor. The business that diversifies its revenue streams and its workforce from the start is the business that survives policy changes, handles Dave taking a week off, and actually achieves the "passive income, generational value" vision all three brothers described.
